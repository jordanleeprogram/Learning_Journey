Project Management

Project Management allows teams to get their product developed efficiently and effectively. There are different types of project management methodologies and concepts that are used within them.

Lifecycles

Lifecycles give a clear path to follow despite how unique all projects are, most teams will use the following set of stages:



Stage one: The Gathering

Will merely involve the gathering of analysis and planning to have a basic concept ready to be produced.


Stage two: The Design

Market research and audience research is conducted for UX and UI designers to then create wireframes and prototypes.
It is then moved the software development team when they are happy with the design.


Stage three: The Implementation

The development team will then create the product using the designs from the previous stage as reference. This is done in a programming language.


Stage four: The Testing

Once a version of the product is completed it goes through rigorous testing by the quality assurance team to find bugs or defects.


Stage five: The Deployment

At this stage the product is ready to be put to use by the consumer.


Stage six: The Maintanence

Once the product is released to a wide base of users, new bugs and defects may appear. Users may also provide feedback for updates or features.
Developers will address these enhancements into place.

There are many different types of project management mehtodologies.
They prioritise different things and go about them in slightly different ways. Here are some examples.

Lean Management

Lean Management aims to reduce waste and add value during each phase of the project.
One of the main priorities is customer satisfaction.
There are 5 princples in place to ensure these standards are met:

Identify value:

To identify value, you need to know who the stakeholders are, and therefore the people you are building the project for.
Once you know who you are making the product for, you can identify how to make your product more .


Map the value stream: Value Stream Mapping (VSM)

VSM is a tool used to create diagrams to visualise your current workflow and your ideal workflow, from initiation to completion.
When comparing the two workflows you can identify any waste during each phase, and remove it.


Create flow:

The project management plan is re-worked to be more efficient once waste from previous stage has been identified and removed.
The team must be included in this process to make sure nothing is missed and all team members are



Establish pull:

Establishing pull means pulling work from the previous process stage as work is completed. Establishing pull can help teams as it keeps work moving seamlessly through the project life cycle.


Continuing improvement:

Lean Management is an iterive process, meaning it is assessed over and over again throughout the process as necessary.
If your stakeholders include external customers, their needs will continue to change as the world around them changes.

Waterfall Management

Waterfall Management is methodology with a linear approach. Each stage of the workflow needs to be completed before moving onto the next.
There are five phases of the Waterfall methodology:


Requirements:

In this stage you gather together the requirements that need to be met in order for the project to be a success. This will include meeting with stakeholders and compiling user research.


Design:

Once the requirements are stated, it is then time to create design solutions.
The design solutions focus on the look of the product as well as also its functionality.
The product must be satisfactory to use and must meet the needs of the stakeholders



Implementation:

The team will come together to decide which design solutions meet the needs of the stakeholders and users.
Once this has been decided, the team will begin to implement the design into a usable product.


Verification:

The team will now test to see whether the product validates the requirements.
If any issues arrive during this stage, the team will work on those issues to come up with solutions.
The product will remain in this stage until stakeholders confirm they are pleased for it to move forward.



Maintanence:

The product will need to be maintained even after it has been deployed.
Such as creating updates to meet security needs, new features or bug fixes. The product will continue in the maintenance phase until the stakeholders say otherwise.

Agile Methodology

An agile methodology is the management of a project by breaking it up into several segments, known as iterations. These are short, constantly repeated cycles with a set of principles to be kept.

It involves constant collaboration and continuous improvement.
The project's team will cycle through planning, executing and evaluating.

This type of project management is advantageous because it allows you to improve the development of the project constantly, with regular checks, testing, moderation and communication between departments.

On the other hand, a drawback is that the the regular testing and different input of different departments can become convoluted, and also takes up a lot of research and time.
